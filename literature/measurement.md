# Measurement and Validation

## Moral Foundations Questionnaire (MFQ / MFQ-2)

- **Graham, J., Haidt, J., & Nosek, B. A. (2009).** *Liberals and conservatives rely on different sets of moral foundations.* JPSP, 96, 1029–1046. — Original MFQ-30.
- **Graham, J., Nosek, B. A., Haidt, J., Iyer, R., Koleva, S., & Ditto, P. H. (2011).** *Mapping the moral domain.* JPSP, 101, 366–385. — Psychometric validation of MFQ-30.
- **Atari, M., Haidt, J., Graham, J., et al. (2023).** *Morality beyond the WEIRD.* JPSP, 125(5), 1157–1188. — Introduces the MFQ-2 with six foundations (splitting Fairness into Equality and Proportionality); validated across 25 populations. The current state of the art.
- **Doğruyol, B., Alper, S., & Yilmaz, O. (2019).** *The five-factor model of the moral foundations theory is stable across WEIRD and non-WEIRD cultures.* Personality and Individual Differences, 151. — Contested finding; recent replications have not always supported.

**Test-retest reliability:** MFQ-30 reports test-retest r in the 0.6–0.8 range over weeks to months in WEIRD samples. MFQ-2 stability data are still accumulating.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Concept doc mentions MFQ as a convergent-validity anchor and acknowledges it "under-performs on predictive validity." Both are accurate. The doc should specify *MFQ-2* not MFQ-30 going forward.

## HEXACO

- **Lee, K., & Ashton, M. C. (2004).** *Psychometric properties of the HEXACO Personality Inventory.* Multivariate Behavioral Research, 39, 329–358. — Original instrument.
- **Ashton, M. C., & Lee, K. (2007).** *Empirical, theoretical, and practical advantages of the HEXACO model of personality structure.* PSPR, 11, 150–166. — Argues honesty-humility (H) is a robust sixth factor missed by the Big Five.
- **Lee, K., & Ashton, M. C. (2018).** *Psychometric properties of the HEXACO-100.* Assessment, 25, 543–556.
- **Henry, S., & Mõttus, R. (2022).** *Test-retest reliability of the HEXACO-100.* PLOS ONE. — 13-day test-retest correlations: median 0.88 (domains), 0.81 (facets), 0.65 (items). Strong stability.

**Concept-doc relevance:** HEXACO honesty-humility is the closest existing personality construct to what patterns-of-choice is trying to measure behaviorally. Convergent validity against H is the *single most informative* benchmark in concept doc's Phase 1. Doc currently references HEXACO once — should be more central in the validation plan.

## Schwartz values theory (PVQ / PVQ-RR)

- **Schwartz, S. H. (1992).** *Universals in the content and structure of values.* Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, 25, 1–65. — 10-value model.
- **Schwartz, S. H., Cieciuch, J., Vecchione, M., et al. (2012).** *Refining the theory of basic individual values.* JPSP, 103, 663–688. — 19-value refined theory.
- **Schwartz, S. H., & Cieciuch, J. (2022).** *Measuring the refined theory of individual values in 49 cultural groups: Psychometrics of the revised Portrait Value Questionnaire.* Assessment, 29, 1005–1019. — Validates PVQ-RR across 49 cultures. 15 of 19 values measured invariantly at metric level across most groups; scalar invariance only across subsets.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Concept doc names Schwartz PVQ as a candidate inventory baseline. PVQ-RR (2022) supersedes the older PVQ-21/PVQ-40 in research use. Note that *scalar invariance is not universal* — comparing absolute Schwartz value levels across cultures is not statistically defensible without group-specific adjustments. This matters if patterns-of-choice tries to do cross-cultural baselining.

## VIA-IS character strengths

- **Peterson, C., & Seligman, M. E. P. (2004).** *Character Strengths and Virtues: A Handbook and Classification.* Oxford UP. — The 24 strengths × 6 virtues classification.
- **Park, N., Peterson, C., & Seligman, M. E. P. (2004).** *Strengths of character and well-being.* Journal of Social and Clinical Psychology, 23, 603–619.
- **McGrath, R. E. (2014).** *Scale- and item-level factor analyses of the VIA Inventory of Strengths.* Assessment, 21, 4–14. — Most thorough psychometric review; factor structure is messy, not the clean 6-virtue solution Peterson/Seligman proposed.
- **VIA-IS psychometrics:** internal consistency α typically > 0.70; 9-month test-retest median r = 0.73.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Concept doc names VIA-IS as a comparison instrument. VIA-IS's factor structure has been disputed; the original 6-virtue grouping does not replicate cleanly. It is still useful as a *strengths-language* inventory but not as a definitive structural model. Flag.

## Dark Triad / SD3

- **Paulhus, D. L., & Williams, K. M. (2002).** *The dark triad of personality.* Journal of Research in Personality, 36, 556–563. — Original construct.
- **Jones, D. N., & Paulhus, D. L. (2014).** *Introducing the Short Dark Triad (SD3).* Assessment, 21, 28–41. — 27-item instrument; widely used.
- **Latham, L., & Stephenson, Z. (2025).** *A critical review of the Short Dark Triad (SD3).* Journal of Criminal Psychology. — Reasonable internal consistency and test-retest; concerns about content validity, especially Machiavellianism vs. psychopathy discriminant validity.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Doc names Dark Triad / SD3 as a discriminant-validity anchor. Reasonable choice — SD3 is brief and widely validated. Be aware of the content-validity concerns; HEXACO low-H captures most of the same variance more cleanly.

## Inclusion of Other in Self (Aron IOS)

- **Aron, A., Aron, E. N., & Smollan, D. (1992).** *Inclusion of Other in the Self Scale and the structure of interpersonal closeness.* JPSP, 63, 596–612. — Single-item Venn-diagram measure.
- **Helgeson, V. S., & Van Vleet, M. (2019).** *Inclusion of Other in Self: An adaptation and exploration in a diverse community sample.* — Found IOS interpretation varies across populations; ~67–86% in college / lab samples interpret "interpersonal connection"; 34–38% in community samples interpret behavior (time together, teamwork).

**Concept-doc relevance:** Doc names IOS as a mediator measure for identity centrality. The instrument is brief and validated, but interpretation is less uniform than the original paper suggested. Use with explicit instructions about what "self" / "other" refer to.

## Behavioral / economic-game instruments

- **Fischbacher, U., & Föllmi-Heusi, F. (2013).** *Lies in disguise—An experimental study on cheating.* Journal of the European Economic Association, 11, 525–547. — Die-roll paradigm; ~39% fully honest, ~22% maximally dishonest. Widely replicated; the most defensible single behavioral measure of dishonesty.
- **Mazar, N., Amir, O., & Ariely, D. (2008).** *The dishonesty of honest people: A theory of self-concept maintenance.* Journal of Marketing Research, 45, 633–644. — Matrix task.
- **Verschuere, B., et al. (2018).** *Registered Replication Report on Mazar, Amir, and Ariely (2008).* AMPPS, 1, 299–317. — 25-site replication, N = 5,786. Effect was *not* replicated; estimate was numerically in the opposite direction of the original.
- **Dictator / ultimatum / public goods / trust games.** Canonical; see Camerer (2003) *Behavioral Game Theory* for the canonical statistics.

**Concept-doc relevance — important:** The doc names "Mazar/Ariely matrix task" as a real-stakes predictive-validity benchmark. The 2018 RRR is decisive: the moral-priming effect *did not replicate*. The matrix task is still usable as a *cheating-quantification* paradigm but should not be cited as evidence that moral primes work. Also: Ariely's broader honesty-research program is partially compromised by the Gino-Ariely connection (see `replication-concerns.md`). Flag inline.

## What's missing

- **Item response theory (IRT) approach.** A forced-choice Bradley-Terry inventory (concept doc §2) effectively is an IRT instrument; literature on Thurstonian IRT for forced-choice (Brown & Maydeu-Olivares 2011, 2018) should anchor the inventory design.
- **Brown, A., & Maydeu-Olivares, A. (2011).** *Item response modeling of forced-choice questionnaires.* Educational and Psychological Measurement, 71, 460–502. — The methodological reference for the inventory layer.
- **Situational Judgment Tests (SJT) literature.** Concept doc's core-narrative scene is functionally an SJT. SJT psychometrics literature (Lievens, Patterson, Christian) is directly applicable but not currently referenced.
