# Behavior Change

## Implementation intentions (Gollwitzer)

- **Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999).** *Implementation intentions: Strong effects of simple plans.* American Psychologist, 54, 493–503. — Original framework: "When [X], I will [Y]."
- **Gollwitzer, P. M., & Sheeran, P. (2006).** *Implementation intentions and goal achievement: A meta-analysis of effects and processes.* Advances in Experimental Social Psychology, 38, 69–119. — 94 independent studies, ~8000 participants, **d = 0.65 medium-to-large effect** on goal attainment. The most heavily cited meta-analysis.
- **Adriaanse, M. A., Vinkers, C. D. W., De Ridder, D. T. D., Hox, J. J., & De Wit, J. B. F. (2011).** *Do implementation intentions help to eat a healthy diet?* Appetite, 56, 183–193. — Meta-analysis specific to eating: d ≈ 0.51 for healthy eating, smaller for unhealthy-eating reduction.
- **Bieleke, M., Keller, L., & Gollwitzer, P. M. (2021).** *If-then planning.* European Review of Social Psychology, 32, 88–122. — Recent integrative review; effect sizes have come down somewhat with better-powered studies but remain medium.

**Relevance:** Concept doc cites Gollwitzer's implementation intentions as "the single highest-leverage software-deliverable intervention" and claims "~0.3–0.6 SD on targeted behaviors." Verified — the d = 0.65 figure for the 2006 meta is the canonical anchor. The concept doc's later claim of "~0.5–0.8 SD on targeted behavior" for the *full stack* (II + practice + identity + accountability) is more speculative — no meta-analysis of *that combination* exists. Flag as plausible-but-unvalidated.

## Identity-based motivation (Oyserman)

- **Oyserman, D., Bybee, D., & Terry, K. (2006).** *Possible selves and academic outcomes: How and when possible selves impel action.* JPSP, 91, 188–204. — Original School-to-Jobs RCT in Detroit; effects on GPA, attendance, behavior.
- **Oyserman, D., & Destin, M. (2010).** *Identity-based motivation: Implications for intervention.* Counseling Psychologist, 38, 1001–1043. — Integrative theoretical paper.
- **Oyserman, D., Lewis, N. A., Yan, V. X., Fisher, O., O'Donnell, S. C., & Horowitz, E. (2017).** *An identity-based motivation framework for self-regulation.* Psychological Inquiry, 28, 139–147.
- **Oyserman, D., Terry, K., & Bybee, D. (2002).** *A possible selves intervention to enhance school involvement.* Journal of Adolescence, 25, 313–326.

**Relevance:** Concept doc invokes Oyserman for "I am X" vs "I value X." The literature supports this: identity-anchored interventions outperform value-anchored ones, especially when difficulty is framed as evidence of importance rather than impossibility. Effect sizes in IBM trials are modest but durable (GPA effects persisting 2 years post-intervention). Concept doc's framing is consistent with the literature.

## Wise interventions (Walton, Yeager)

- **Walton, G. M., & Cohen, G. L. (2011).** *A brief social-belonging intervention improves academic and health outcomes of minority students.* Science, 331, 1447–1451. — 1-hour intervention; halved minority achievement gap over 3 years.
- **Walton, G. M., & Wilson, T. D. (2018).** *Wise interventions: Psychological remedies for social and personal problems.* Psychological Review, 125, 617–655. — Theoretical synthesis.
- **Walton, G. M., et al. (2023).** *Where and with whom does a brief social-belonging intervention promote progress in college?* Science, 380, 499–505. — Multi-site replication across 22 colleges, N = ~26,000. Effects depend on context — works where belonging affordances exist.
- **Yeager, D. S., Hanselman, P., Walton, G. M., et al. (2019).** *A national experiment reveals where a growth mindset improves achievement.* Nature, 573, 364–369. — Growth-mindset effect d ≈ 0.03 average, with heterogeneity by school context.

**Relevance — important:** Walton/Yeager's recent work is *the* model for how character-lab should think about effect sizes and context-dependence. The 2019 *Nature* mindset paper and 2023 *Science* belonging paper both show: small average effects, large heterogeneity, *context affordances matter as much as the intervention*. This should shape character-lab's effect-size honesty in marketing (concept doc Open Questions §8). Flag inline.

## Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan)

- **Deci, E. L. (1971).** *Effects of externally mediated rewards on intrinsic motivation.* JPSP, 18, 105–115. — Original overjustification finding.
- **Deci, E. L., Koestner, R., & Ryan, R. M. (1999).** *A meta-analytic review of experiments examining the effects of extrinsic rewards on intrinsic motivation.* Psychological Bulletin, 125, 627–668. — 128 studies; tangible rewards undermine intrinsic motivation, d ≈ -0.34.
- **Cameron, J., & Pierce, W. D. (1994).** *Reinforcement, reward, and intrinsic motivation: A meta-analysis.* Review of Educational Research, 64, 363–423. — Contested Deci's earlier findings; argued the effect is small and inconsequential. Was itself methodologically critiqued.
- **Peters, J. R., et al. (2022).** *An evaluation of the overjustification hypothesis: A replication of Deci (1971).* — First direct replication; statistically non-significant results.
- **Ryan, R. M., & Deci, E. L. (2000).** *Intrinsic and extrinsic motivations: Classic definitions and new directions.* Contemporary Educational Psychology, 25, 54–67. — Canonical SDT statement.

**Relevance — flag this:** Concept doc's claim "Streaks for 'honesty days' corrupt motivation via the crowding-out effect (Deci 1971+)" is more contested than the prose implies. The Deci-Cameron exchange is ongoing; the 2022 Peters replication did not find the effect; published-vs-unpublished imbalances are similar to other crowding-out paradigms. The *direction* (intrinsic > extrinsic for moral domains) remains the consensus view; the *magnitude and reliability* are contested. Flag inline. See `replication-concerns.md`.

## Habit formation (Wood)

- **Lally, P., Van Jaarsveld, C. H. M., Potts, H. W. W., & Wardle, J. (2010).** *How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the real world.* European Journal of Social Psychology, 40, 998–1009. — Median 66 days to automaticity for simple daily behaviors; range 18–254. "21 days to form a habit" is folk wisdom.
- **Wood, W., & Neal, D. T. (2007).** *A new look at habits and the habit–goal interface.* Psychological Review, 114, 843–863. — Habits become decoupled from goals after sufficient repetition with consistent context.
- **Wood, W., & Rünger, D. (2016).** *Psychology of habit.* Annual Review of Psychology, 67, 289–314. — Recent canonical review.
- **Wood, W. (2019).** *Good Habits, Bad Habits.* — Trade book; useful framing of context-stability triad.

**Relevance:** Concept doc's daily-bookend check-ins and contextual implementation intentions implicitly target habit formation. Lally's 66-day median is the right anchor; the "weeks 4–12 intervention activation" plan is consistent with what the literature would predict for habit-establishment timelines. Single direct addition: concept doc could mention that *contextual stability* is the under-appreciated variable — same situations, same time of day matters more than effort.

## Transtheoretical Model (Prochaska)

- **Prochaska, J. O., & DiClemente, C. C. (1983).** *Stages and processes of self-change of smoking.* JCCP, 51, 390–395. — Original.
- **Prochaska, J. O., & Velicer, W. F. (1997).** *The transtheoretical model of health behavior change.* American Journal of Health Promotion, 12, 38–48. — Canonical TTM statement.
- **Aveyard, P., et al. (2009).** *The effectiveness of smoking cessation interventions based on transtheoretical model.* — Found stage-matching did not improve outcomes vs. one-size-fits-all.
- **West, R. (2005).** *Time for a change: Putting the transtheoretical (stages of change) model to rest.* Addiction, 100, 1036–1039. — Forceful critique.

**Relevance — flag this:** Concept doc treats TTM stage-matching as established ("stage-matching matters most"). The empirical literature on TTM stage-matching is more skeptical: West (2005) called for its retirement; multiple stage-matched-intervention RCTs (smoking, alcohol) failed to outperform non-matched controls. The *descriptive distinction* (precontemplation through maintenance) is fine; the *prescriptive claim* (matching interventions to stage improves outcomes) is weaker than the doc implies. Flag inline.

## What else should be cited

- **Bandura, A. (1977).** *Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral change.* Psychological Review, 84, 191–215. — Foundational; not currently in references.
- **Bandura, A. (1997).** *Self-efficacy: The exercise of control.* Freeman. — Comprehensive treatment.
- **Duhigg, C. (2012).** *The Power of Habit.* — Popular synthesis; useful only as a popular reference, not academic.
- **Milkman, K. L., et al. (2021).** *Megastudy.* Nature, 597, 481–486. — Large coordinated test of 53 nudge interventions for gym attendance; most had small to null effects. Relevant honesty-anchor for effect-size expectations.
- **Hagger, M. S., et al. (2016).** *A multilab preregistered replication of the ego-depletion effect.* Perspectives on Psychological Science, 11, 546–573. — Failed to replicate ego depletion. Concept doc should *avoid* leaning on ego-depletion framings if it does.
