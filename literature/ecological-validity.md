# Ecological Validity: Lab vs. Life in Moral Psychology

The single biggest credibility risk for patterns-of-choice is the "it's just a game" problem. The literature here is concerning.

## Hypothetical vs. real-life moral choices

- **Bostyn, D. H., Sevenhant, S., & Roets, A. (2018).** *Of mice, men, and trolleys: Hypothetical judgment versus real-life behavior in trolley-style moral dilemmas.* Psychological Science, 29, 1084–1093. — First real-stakes trolley study. Hypothetical responses did *not* predict real-life choices; participants were ~2× more willing to shock a single mouse to save five when the decision was actually consequential than in the hypothetical version. Affective and cognitive correlates *did* track between hypothetical and real.
- **FeldmanHall, O., Mobbs, D., Evans, D., Hiscox, L., Navrady, L., & Dalgleish, T. (2012).** *What we say and what we do: The relationship between real and hypothetical moral choices.* Cognition, 123, 434–441. — Earlier finding: real-stakes harm decisions diverge from hypothetical reports.
- **Bauman, C. W., McGraw, A. P., Bartels, D. M., & Warren, C. (2014).** *Revisiting external validity: Concerns about trolley problems and other sacrificial dilemmas in moral psychology.* Social and Personality Psychology Compass, 8, 536–554. — Methodological critique of trolley dilemmas as moral-psychology research tools.
- **Colman, A. M., Gold, N., & Pulford, B. D. (2019).** *Comparing hypothetical and real-life trolley problems: Commentary on Bostyn, Sevenhant, and Roets (2018).* Psychological Science, 30, 1380–1383. — Counter-perspective: argues the lab-real divergence is partly an artifact of stake asymmetry and small-N.

**Concept-doc relevance — critical:** This is the single most important piece of literature for patterns-of-choice. The doc's premise — that low-stakes choices reveal something about high-stakes behavior — is *contested* by Bostyn/FeldmanHall. The doc's mitigation (cost-of-virtue probe, real-stakes nights, informant reports) is exactly the right response. But the doc should be explicit that the burden of proof is on patterns-of-choice to *empirically demonstrate* that its low-stakes measures track real-life behavior — this is not an assumption that can be made. Flag as a foundational empirical question, not a side concern.

## Mazar/Ariely matrix task failure

- **Verschuere, B., Meijer, E. H., Jim, A., et al. (2018).** *Registered Replication Report on Mazar, Amir, and Ariely (2008).* AMPPS, 1, 299–317. — 25-lab pre-registered replication, N = 5,786. The moral-priming effect (Ten Commandments → less cheating) was numerically in the *opposite* direction of the original; 95% CI included zero. The single most cited "moral priming reduces cheating" finding in behavioral economics did not replicate.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Doc lists "Mazar/Ariely matrix task" as a real-stakes predictive-validity benchmark. The matrix task itself is fine as a cheating-measurement instrument (it produces real, varied behavior). What does *not* hold up is the broader claim that moral primes modulate cheating in it. Flag inline.

## Die-roll cheating paradigm

- **Fischbacher, U., & Föllmi-Heusi, F. (2013).** *Lies in disguise.* JEEA, 11, 525–547. — Original.
- **Abeler, J., Nosenzo, D., & Raymond, C. (2019).** *Preferences for truth-telling.* Econometrica, 87, 1115–1153. — Meta-analysis of 90 dishonesty-game experiments, total N = ~44,000. Shows people lie much less than monetary incentives would predict; the underreporting is robust across cultures and stake sizes.
- **Gerlach, P., Teodorescu, K., & Hertwig, R. (2019).** *The truth about lies: A meta-analysis on dishonest behavior.* Psychological Bulletin, 145, 1–44.

**Concept-doc relevance:** The die-roll paradigm is one of the few robustly-replicated moral-behavior measures in the literature. Strong candidate for the cost-of-virtue probe's anchoring lab task. Concept doc names it in Phase 1; this is well-placed.

## Informant reports

- **Vazire, S. (2010).** *Who knows what about a person? The self-other knowledge asymmetry (SOKA) model.* JPSP, 98, 281–300. — Informants are *more* accurate than self-report on observable, evaluative traits (e.g., honesty, agreeableness); selves are more accurate on internal states.
- **Connelly, B. S., & Ones, D. S. (2010).** *An other perspective on personality: Meta-analytic integration of observers' accuracy and predictive validity.* Psychological Bulletin, 136, 1092–1122. — Multi-informant ratings *outperform* self-report for predicting behavior in honesty, conscientiousness, agreeableness.
- **Funder, D. C. (2012).** *Accurate personality judgment.* Current Directions in Psychological Science, 21, 177–182.

**Concept-doc relevance — strong support:** Concept doc's claim that informant reports are "probably the single most important credibility move" is well-supported. The Vazire SOKA model is directly relevant: honesty-humility is *the* most-evaluative personality trait, and is *exactly* where informant > self. This should be the strongest validation arm of Phase 2.

## Ecological momentary assessment / experience sampling

- **Hofmann, W., Wisneski, D. C., Brandt, M. J., & Skitka, L. J. (2014).** *Morality in everyday life.* Science, 345, 1340–1343. — Experience sampling of ~1,250 participants, 13,240 morally relevant events. Established that everyday morality is dominated by mundane events (gossip, fairness, loyalty) rather than dramatic dilemmas.
- **Mehl, M. R., & Conner, T. S. (Eds.). (2012).** *Handbook of Research Methods for Studying Daily Life.* — Methodological canon.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Hofmann 2014 is foundational support for the concept doc's choice of small, frequent, mundane scenarios over dramatic trolley-style dilemmas. The doc should reference this paper directly — it's the strongest empirical justification for the "small scenes, daily, weeks-long" design.

## What's missing from concept doc

- **Hofmann's everyday-morality empirical baseline** — directly supports the design choice but isn't cited.
- **Funder/Vazire informant-validity work** — supports the doc's strongest validation arm but isn't cited.
- **Abeler meta-analysis on die-roll honesty** — supports the cost-of-virtue probe with cleaner numbers than Mazar/Ariely.
