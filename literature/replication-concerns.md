# Replication and Credibility Crisis in Moral Psychology

A field-specific lens: which flagship findings in moral psychology have *not* replicated.

## The Francesca Gino / Dan Ariely fraud cases

- **Data Colada (Uri Simonsohn, Leif Nelson, Joe Simmons). (2021–2023).** *Series on Francesca Gino.* https://datacolada.org/ — Identified data anomalies in 4 Gino-authored papers on dishonesty research.
- **Harvard Business School (2023).** Internal investigation determined Gino "committed research misconduct intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly." Harvard revoked her tenure in May 2025 — the first such action since the 1940s.
- **Ariely, D., Mazar, N., Bazerman, M., & others.** "Signing-at-the-top reduces honesty" finding (Shu, Mazar, Gino, Bazerman, Ariely 2012) was retracted in 2021–2023; data fabrication was identified in both the Ariely-collected dataset and a separate Gino-collected dataset.

**Concept-doc relevance — important:** A non-trivial portion of the "moral primes reduce cheating" literature that the concept doc draws on indirectly is from labs now under suspicion. The doc explicitly names "Mazar/Ariely matrix task" as a benchmark — the *task* is fine (it produces real cheating behavior) but the *findings using the task* are now to be treated as unconfirmed. Flag inline. See also `ecological-validity.md` for the 2018 RRR.

## Moral self-licensing

- **Blanken, I., van de Ven, N., & Zeelenberg, M. (2015).** *A meta-analytic review of moral licensing.* PSPB, 41, 540–558. — 91 studies, N = 7,397. Estimated effect d = 0.31. Published-vs-unpublished imbalance suggests inflation.
- **Blanken, I., et al.** *Three attempts to replicate the moral licensing effect.* Social Psychology — Failed to replicate Sachdeva et al. (2009) across student, MTurk samples (N up to 940). Concluded no strong support for the moral self-regulation framework as originally specified.
- **Mullen, E., & Monin, B. (2016).** *Consistency versus licensing effects of past moral behavior.* Annual Review of Psychology, 67, 363–385. — Both effects (licensing and consistency) appear; predicting which depends on framing and identity stability.
- **Rotella, A., Jung, J., Chinn, C., & Barclay, P. (2025).** *Observation moderates the moral licensing effect: A meta-analytic test.* PSPB. — More recent meta finds observation conditions matter substantially.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Concept doc cites moral self-licensing as a real risk and proposes mitigations (tight tether to daily life). The phenomenon is *real but smaller and more context-dependent* than the original Monin & Miller (2001) and Sachdeva et al. (2009) papers implied. The doc's framing ("watch for it") is appropriate; no inline flag needed, but the *magnitude* of the concern should be calibrated downward.

## Overjustification / crowding-out

- **Deci, E. L., Koestner, R., & Ryan, R. M. (1999).** *Meta-analytic review.* Psychological Bulletin, 125, 627–668. — Tangible rewards undermine intrinsic motivation, d ≈ -0.34.
- **Cameron, J., & Pierce, W. D. (1994).** *Reinforcement, reward, and intrinsic motivation: A meta-analysis.* Review of Educational Research, 64, 363–423. — Contested Deci's claims; argued effect is small.
- **Peters, J. R., et al. (2022).** *An evaluation of the overjustification hypothesis: A replication of Deci (1971).* — First direct replication of the Deci 1971 study. Statistically non-significant.

**Concept-doc relevance — flag this inline:** Concept doc states "Streaks for 'honesty days' corrupt motivation via the crowding-out effect (Deci 1971+)." The directionality (extrinsic rewards in moral domains are concerning) is reasonable; the *empirical confidence* is more contested than the prose implies. The 2022 direct replication failure is recent enough that the doc author may not have seen it.

## Ego depletion (cautionary tale)

- **Hagger, M. S., Chatzisarantis, N. L. D., Alberts, H., et al. (2016).** *A multilab preregistered replication of the ego-depletion effect.* PPS, 11, 546–573. — 23 labs, total N = ~2,141. Effect size in coordinated replication was essentially zero.
- **Vohs, K. D., Schmeichel, B. J., Lohmann, S., et al. (2021).** *A multi-site preregistered paradigmatic test of the ego depletion effect.* Psychological Science. — Another large replication, weak/null effects.

**Concept-doc relevance:** Concept doc does not currently lean on ego-depletion framings, which is good. If future revisions are tempted to invoke "moral self-control as a finite resource" — don't. The construct is in serious empirical trouble.

## Embodied cognition / priming effects

- **Doyen, S., Klein, O., Pichon, C.-L., & Cleeremans, A. (2012).** *Behavioral priming: It's all in the mind, but whose mind?* PLOS ONE, 7, e29081. — Failed replication of Bargh et al.'s elderly-priming → slow walking effect.
- **Verschuere et al. (2018) RRR on Mazar/Ariely 2008** — moral priming with Ten Commandments did not replicate.

**Concept-doc relevance:** The doc's quick-fire round and order/anchor effects probe context-dependence of moral choice. This is fine empirically — *order effects on choice* do replicate. What does *not* replicate is the broader social-priming program (e.g., subtle cues changing later behavior in unrelated tasks). Stay narrow.

## What has replicated well in moral psychology

For balance — not everything is in trouble. Robustly replicated:

- **Die-roll honesty paradigms** (Abeler et al. 2019 meta, N = ~44,000) — people lie much less than incentives predict; effect is cross-culturally robust.
- **Loss aversion and inequity aversion** in economic games — well-replicated.
- **Implementation intentions** (Gollwitzer & Sheeran 2006, d ≈ 0.65) — recent meta-analyses report somewhat smaller but still real effects.
- **Prosocial spending → well-being** (Aknin et al. 2013, 2022) — recent direct replications confirm.
- **Self-other harm asymmetry** (Crockett et al. 2014) — robust.
- **WEIRD-vs-non-WEIRD differences in game behavior** (Henrich et al. 2010 and follow-ups).

## What's missing from concept doc

- Explicit acknowledgment that **the most prominent ethics-research lab (Ariely) and an adjacent honesty-research lab (Gino) are partially or wholly in disrepute** — patterns-of-choice's "research-grade" positioning needs to be aware of this. The doc's emphasis on pre-registration and open science is the correct response, but should be tied to *why* — naming the post-Gino landscape directly is one way to signal that patterns-of-choice knows the standards have changed.
