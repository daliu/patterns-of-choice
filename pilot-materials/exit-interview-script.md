# Exit Interview Script — Pilot (Template)

**Status:** Draft. 45-minute structured interview conducted at the end of each participant's 4-week pilot. The most-leveraged single conversation per participant — surfaces the qualitative findings that weekly check-ins don't quite get to, plus formal close-out.

**Note to researcher:** Have the participant's full session log and weekly-interview notes available. Reference specific scenarios by name when you ask about them. Don't surface aggregated scores — even at exit, the pilot doesn't show analytical results to participants.

---

## Opening (60 seconds)

> "This is the exit interview for the pilot — about 45 minutes. There are five sections: usability, scenarios, honesty-of-self-reporting, wellbeing, and a final open section. As with the weekly calls, you can decline any question, or stop the conversation at any point.
>
> Audio is recording — okay to continue?"

(Same audio recording-consent norms as weekly calls. Transcription is automated; raw audio deleted within 30 days; transcript de-identified before any researcher beyond the interviewer sees it.)

---

## Section 1: Usability (10 minutes)

**Lead-in:**

> "I want to start with the experience of using the app over four weeks. Walk me through your typical session pattern — what time of day, where you were, what you were doing immediately before opening it."

(Wait. This is data — when and where the app fits into life is part of whether the design works.)

**Follow-up prompts (use as needed):**

- "Were there sessions that felt easy and natural, and sessions that felt like work? What was different about them?"
- "What's the smallest thing that would have made you quit?" (This is a key item; let them answer fully before moving on.)
- "Were there days you skipped? What was the situation that made you skip?"
- "How did you feel right after closing the app on a typical day — energized, drained, neutral, something else?"
- "If a friend asked you to describe what doing this study was like, what would you say?"

**Listening for:**
- Embedded triggers and their durability (morning coffee, before bed, during commute — these are the candidates for default reminder timing)
- Specific exit threats (lengthening sessions, certain scenario types, specific UI friction)
- Post-session mood signal (drain is a real risk; energized is the engagement-without-burnout marker)
- Self-description of the experience — surfaces the participant's mental model of what they were doing

---

## Section 2: Scenario interpretability (15 minutes)

**Lead-in:**

> "I want to pick three scenarios from your history and ask you to re-read them with me. I'll show you the scenario text, you tell me what you remember about it and what you'd do now — including whether your answer would be the same."

(Researcher pre-selects 3 scenarios per participant: one quick-fire item, one branching-narrative scene, one cost-of-virtue probe. Mix of domains.)

**Per scenario:**

1. Read the scenario aloud (or show on screen).
2. "What does this scenario ask, in your own words?"
3. "Do you remember what you picked? If you don't, that's fine — what do you think you'd pick now?"
4. (After they answer): show them what they actually picked. "Does that surprise you?"
5. "Looking at it now, is there anything about the scenario itself that feels off — unrealistic, biased, missing important context, set up to push you toward one answer?"

**Listening for:**
- Interpretability mismatches (their re-narration doesn't match the scenario's intent)
- Choice instability (re-choice differs from logged choice without obvious reason — signal that the scenario lacks a stable answer)
- Edit signals (specific complaints that should feed editorial review of the scenario corpus)
- Framing bias (scenarios that the participant feels push them toward an answer — these compromise the measurement)

**Per scenario, take notes on:**
- Re-narration accuracy (full / partial / off)
- Re-choice consistency (same / different / unsure)
- Scenario flag (none / wording / framing / structural)

---

## Section 3: Honesty of self-reporting (10 minutes)

**This is the most delicate section. Take time.**

**Lead-in:**

> "I want to ask you about something that's hard to talk about directly. The app is built on the assumption that what you click is something close to what you'd actually do in those situations. I want to know whether that was true for you — specifically, whether there were moments where you picked something that wasn't really what you'd do."

(Pause. Let them respond on their own time.)

**Follow-up prompts (use as needed):**

- "Were there scenarios where you picked an option because it sounded like the kind of person you'd want to be, even though you weren't sure that's what you'd actually do?"
- "Were there scenarios where you picked the option you thought the app was 'expecting' — even if you don't really know what the app expects?"
- "Were there moments where you felt the system was watching you in a way that changed your answer?"
- "If you'd been doing this on paper, alone, with no one ever seeing it — would you have answered any of them differently?"

**Listening for:**
- Social-desirability bias (the most likely confound; a participant who can name specific cases is more reliable overall than one who claims they always answered honestly)
- Demand characteristics (participant has built a model of what the app is measuring; their answers may be shaped by that model)
- Identity-shaped answers (the question "would the person I want to be choose X?" turning into the primary decision criterion, even when the scenario asked "what would you actually do?")
- Refusal-to-engage (participant clams up on this topic; itself a signal worth flagging)

**Important:** participants who acknowledge inflated answers should NOT feel they've ruined the data. The honest acknowledgment IS data. Reassure them.

---

## Section 4: Wellbeing check (5 minutes)

**Lead-in:**

> "Almost done. I want to ask about how the four weeks went for you, beyond the app itself. Has anything about doing this study affected your mood, your anxiety, how you see yourself — in any direction, positive or negative?"

**Follow-up prompts:**

- "If yes — describe?"
- "Did anything come up that you wished you could talk to someone about, beyond the weekly calls with me?"
- "Would you recommend this study to a friend? Why or why not?"
- "Is there anything we should have warned you about that we didn't?"

**Listening for:**
- Distress signals (mood drop, anxiety escalation, sleep disruption attributed to study)
- Scrupulosity onset (constant self-monitoring beyond the sessions)
- Relationship effects (partners or family noticing changes; conversations about the study with people close)
- Resource gaps (the participant wishes they had access to therapy, peer support, or specific information the study didn't provide)

**If anything concerning surfaces:** stop the structured interview. Use the language: *"I'm going to step out of the script for a moment. Tell me more about that. We can pause the recording if you'd like."*

After the conversation, provide:
- Local crisis resources (988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline in the US; equivalents in other locales)
- Affirmation that the participant did not "break" the study by surfacing distress
- Offer of withdrawal with full compensation (not prorated) as a no-questions-asked option

---

## Section 5: Open (5 minutes)

**Lead-in:**

> "Last section is open. Is there anything you wished I'd asked you that I didn't?"

(Wait. This is the highest-signal moment in the interview. Most participants will hesitate; resist filling the silence.)

**If nothing surfaces, gentle prompts:**

- "Anything you've been thinking about during these four weeks that you haven't had a chance to say?"
- "Is there a question the app should ask that it doesn't?"
- "Was there anything in your real life this month that the app should have given you a way to talk about, that it didn't?"

**At the end:**

> "Thank you. The pilot ends here for you. Compensation will be processed within 14 days. Your data will be deleted within 90 days unless you've consented to keeping the anonymized transcript. If anything you said today changes — if you remember something, or if you want to withdraw something — email me by [date 14 days out]. After that we're done."

---

## After the call

Within 4 hours:

1. Mark recording's anonymization status
2. Compile per-participant scenario-flag list for editorial review
3. Note any urgent flags from Sections 3 or 4

Within 7 days:

4. Transcribe and de-identify; delete raw audio
5. Update the cross-participant pattern-tracker (recurring scenario issues, recurring usability problems, recurring wellbeing signals)
6. Process compensation through the chosen platform

Within 30 days of all exit interviews complete:

7. Cross-participant qualitative analysis
8. Decision document: GO / NO-GO / MIXED for advancing to OSF lock per `../pilot-protocol.md` §"Decision criteria for proceeding to OSF lock"

---

## What the exit interview is for, structurally

Most of the pilot's *quantitative* data (completion rates, scenario flag counts, response times) is already in the session log by exit time. The exit interview produces three things the log can't:

1. **Honest acknowledgment of social-desirability bias.** Section 3 is the only structured opportunity in the pilot for participants to surface the gap between their clicks and what they'd actually do. Quantitative analysis is meaningless without an estimate of how often this gap is large.

2. **Wellbeing-adverse-event surfacing.** Section 4 catches the participants for whom the pilot was genuinely worse-for-them, not just demanding. This is required for credible adverse-events reporting in the eventual published write-up.

3. **The "wished I'd asked" prompt.** Section 5 surfaces qualitative findings that wouldn't have emerged through any structured question. Several past behavioral-study reports document Section-5-equivalent insights as the most-cited findings in eventual write-ups.

The exit interview is therefore the highest-leverage 45 minutes per participant. Pay attention to it.
